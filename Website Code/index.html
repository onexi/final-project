Data Visualization: Popular Songs and their Lyrics

The following visualizations are made from a dataset containing the lyrics of the Billboard Top 100 songs in the US of every year since 1965.

Song Length - number of words in the song

This is the simplest parameter: how many words does your song have? As you can see, the general trend shows an increase in the songs' lyrics' lengths over the years, with 2003 and 2006 the peak years for average song length.

Song Length (individual songs)

Song Length (average over the Top 100 of each year)

Vocabulary Richness - number of different words in the song

Another simple parameter: how many different words do your lyrics contain? Again, the general trend is an increase in the number of different words used per song, with 2003 another peak year. Similar to the song length, a significant decrease can be observed since 2003.

Vocabulary Richness (individual songs)

Vocabulary Richness (average over the Top 100 of each year)

Words most Frequently Used

The following word cloud displays the 100 words used the most in popular songs. This parameter in the prediction measures how many of the 100 words each song contains. Since there is no filtering on the popular words, it is not surprising to find "you", "I" and "the" as the most frequently used words in lyrics.

Themes of a song - how many words reference each topic

Every song has an overarching theme or message! Using wordbanks for each main topic (music, lifestyle, party, love, death and religion), we can count how many words of the song reference these topics and estimate the main theme of the song. The plot shows the dominance of love themes in popular songs, while religion and death are less popular topics for lyricists.

Use of Vulgarity - number of "vulgar" words per song

Vulgarity is increasingly present in popular songs, especially in genres such as hip hop. The graph below shows the average number of "vulgar" words used per song over the top 100 of each year. 2004 was a peak year for vulgarity in the Billboard US Top 100, and it seems 2017 will reach a similar level.

Sentiment of the song - happy or sad?

Must a song be happy for it to be popular? What emotions are lyricists trying to convey? The next chart shows a simplified analysis of the sentiment/modd of the songs every year. Overall, there are about twice as more "happy" songs than "sad" ones every year, and the trend seems to be constant over the years.

Use of Repetiton - number of words repeted at least 4 times

Do you ever feel that a song is beating you to death with a chorus line repeted 10 times? This is a common strategy for lyrics writers, repetition helps listeners memorize the lyrics and creates greater impact. This graph shows how many words per song on average are repeted at least 4 times. The condition that the words be at least 5 characters long is to avoid frequent words such as "I", "you", "a", "the", etc. to be taken into consideraton.
